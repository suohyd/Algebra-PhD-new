    Section 1.3 Group algebra

    Definition 1.3.1. Group Ring.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with unity and \(G\) a group. Consider the following set.
    \begin{equation*} RG=\{\phi\in\Hom{Sets}{G}{R}:\phi(g)=0\quad\text{for all but finitely many }\,g\in G\} \end{equation*}
    Define ‘addition’ and ‘scalar multiplication’ in \(RG\) as follows.
    \begin{equation*} (\phi r+\psi s)(g)=\phi(g)r+\psi(g)s \end{equation*}
    The ‘multiplication’ in \(RG\) is defined as convolution product.
    \begin{equation*} (\phi\psi)(g)=\sum \phi(h)\psi(h^{-1}g)\quad\text{such that}\quad h\in G,\text{ and } \phi(h)\psi(h^{-1}g)\neq 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.3.2.

    Show that \(RG\) is closed under addition, scalar multiplication, and it is an \(R\)-module. Furthermore, show that multiplication is bilinear.
    🔗
    🔗
    We now proceed to check the associativity of multiplication and the existence of unity in \(RG\text{.}\) We first observe that \(RG\) is a free \(R\)-module.
    🔗

    Proposition 1.3.3.

    Keep notations of Definition 1.3.1 above. Let for \(g\in G\text{,}\) \(\chi_g\) be the characteristic function of \(g\text{,}\) i.e., \(\chi_g(h)=0\) if \(h\neq g\) and \(\chi_g(g)=1\text{.}\) Then, set \(\{\chi_g:g\in G\}\) is an \(R\)-basis of \(RG\text{.}\) In particular, \(RG\) is a free \(R\)-module.
    🔗
    Furthermore, \(G\to RG\) given by \(g\mapsto\chi_g\) is a monoid homomorphism. In particular, \(\chi_g\chi_h=\chi_{gh}\) and if \(e\in G\) is the identity then, \(\chi_e\) is the unity in \(RG\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose that \(\sum\chi_{g_i}r_i=0\) for some \(g_i\in G\) and \(r_i\in R\text{.}\) Evaluating at \(g_j\text{,}\) we get \(r_j=0\text{.}\) Thus, the set is linearly independent. Now, let \(\phi\in RG\text{.}\) Then, we can write \(\phi=\sum\chi_g\phi(g)\) where the sum is over all \(g\in G\) such that \(\phi(g)\neq 0\text{.}\) This shows that the set spans \(RG\text{.}\) Thus, it is an \(R\)-basis of \(RG\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Direct calculation shows that \(\chi_g\chi_h=\chi_{gh}\) for all \(g,h\in G\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We show the associativity of multiplication. Let \(\phi,\psi,\zeta\in RG\text{.}\) Then, there exists \(r_i,s_j,t_k\in R\) such that \(\phi=\sum\chi_{g_i}r_i\text{,}\) \(\psi=\sum\chi_{g_j}s_j\text{,}\) \(\zeta=\sum\chi_{g_k}t_k\text{.}\) Therefore, we obtain the following.
    \begin{align*} \left(\phi\psi\right)\zeta\amp =\left(\left(\sum\chi_{g_i}r_i\right)\left(\sum\chi_{g_j}s_j\right)\right)\sum\chi_{g_k}t_k\\ \amp = \sum\left((\chi_{g_i}\chi_{g_j})\chi_{g_k}\right)(r_is_jt_k)\\ \amp =\sum\chi_{g_i}\left(\chi_{g_j}\chi_{g_k}\right)(r_is_jt_k) \\ \amp =\phi\left(\psi\zeta\right) \end{align*}
    This shows the associativity of multiplication.
    🔗
    For any \(\chi_{g_i}a_i\in RG\) we get the following.
    \begin{align*} \left((\chi_{g_i}a_i)\chi_e\right)(h) \amp =\sum\chi_{g_i}a_i(x)\chi_e(x^{-1}h) \end{align*}
    The above equation is nonzero only when \(x=g_i\) and \(x^{-1}h=e\text{.}\) This implies that \(h=g_i\text{.}\) Thus, we get \(\left((\chi_{g_i}a_i)\chi_e\right)(h)=\chi_{g_i}a_i(h)\text{.}\) Similarly, we can show that \(\left(\chi_e(\chi_{g_i}a_i)\right)(h)=\chi_{g_i}a_i(h)\text{.}\) This shows that \(\chi_e\) is the unity in \(RG\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.3.4. Group algebra of the cyclic group of order 2.

    Suppose that \(C_2=\{\pm 1\}\) denotes the cyclic group of order two. The general element of \(\Z C_2\) is of the form \(a\chi_1 + b\chi_{-1}\) for some \(a,b\in\Z\text{.}\) Observe that \(\chi_{-1}^k=\chi_{(-1)^k}\text{,}\) and that \(\Z C_2\) is commutative. Consider the map \(\varphi=\Z[x]\to\Z C_2\) given as follows.
    \begin{equation*} a_0+a_1x+\cdots+a_nx^n\mapsto a_0\chi_1+a_1\chi_{-1}+a_2\chi_{(-1)^2}+\cdots+a_n\chi_{(-1)^n} \end{equation*}
    Check that the above map is a surjective homomorphism of rings. We compute its kernel.
    \begin{equation*} \ker\varphi=\left\{\sum_{i=0}^n a_ix^i\in\Z[x]: a_0\chi_1+a_1\chi_{-1}+\cdots+a_n\chi_{(-1)^n}=0\right\} \end{equation*}
    We have \(x^2-1\in\ker\varphi\text{.}\) We claim that the kernel is generated by \(x^2-1\text{.}\) Let \(f(x)=\sum_{i=0}^n a_ix^i\in\ker\varphi\text{.}\) Then, \(\left(\sum_{k\text{ even}}a_k\right)\chi_1+\left(\sum_{\ell\text{ odd}}a_\ell\right)\chi_{-1}=0\text{.}\) As \(\{\chi_1,\chi_{-1}\}\) is a \(\Z\) basis we have that \(\sum_{k\text{ even}}a_k=0\) and \(\sum_{\ell\text{ odd}}a_\ell=0\text{.}\) Note that \(f(1)=\sum_{k\text{ even}}a_k+\sum_{\ell\text{ odd}}a_\ell=0\text{,}\) and that \(\sum_{k\text{ even}}a_k-\sum_{\ell\text{ odd}}a_\ell=0\text{.}\) Hence, \(x-1\) and \(x+1\) divides \(f(x)\text{.}\) As \(x-1\) and \(x+1\) are coprime, we get that \((x-1)(x+1)=x^2-1\) divides \(f(x)\text{.}\) This shows that \(\ker\varphi=(x^2-1)\text{.}\) By the first isomorphism theorem, \(\varphi\) induces isomorphism of rings \(\Z[x]/(x^2-1)\cong\Z C_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.3.7.

    Suppose that \(G\) is a group and that \(R\) is a commutative ring. Then, \(RG\) is an \(R\)-algebra, and it is a free \(R\)-module with basis \(\{\chi_g\mid g\in G\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(A\) be an \(R\)-algebra, and let \(\phi\colon G\to A\) be a homomorphism to the multiplicative monoid of \(A\text{.}\) Then, \(\phi\) extends uniquely to an \(R\)-algebra homomorphism \(\phi\colon RG\to A\) of \(R\)-algebras.
    🔗
    In other words, the following diagram commutes.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    In view of Proposition 1.3.3, it suffices to show the universal property. Let \(A\) be an \(R\)-algebra and let \(\phi\colon G\to A\) be a homomorphism to the multiplicative monoid of \(A\text{.}\) Define \(\tilde{\phi}\colon RG\to A\) as follows.
    \begin{equation*} \tilde{\phi}\left(\sum\chi_gr_g\right)=\sum\phi(g)r_g \end{equation*}
    We leave it to the reader to check that \(\tilde{\phi}\) is a well-defined homomorphism of \(R\)-algebras and that it is the unique extension of \(\phi\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.3.9. Augmentation Ideal.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with unity and \(G\) a nontrivial group. The augmentation map is the \(R\)-algebra homomorphism \(\epsilon:RG\to R\) given by \(\epsilon(\sum\chi_gr_g)=\sum r_g\text{.}\) The kernel of the augmentation map is called the augmentation ideal of \(RG\text{,}\) and it is denoted by \(I(G)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.3.10. Augmentation map is an \(R\)-algebra homomorphism.

    Show that the augmentation map \(\epsilon:RG\to R\) is a well-defined surjective \(R\)-algebra homomorphism.
