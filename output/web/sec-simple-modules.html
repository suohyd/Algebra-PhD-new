    Section 3.2 Simple modules

    We define simple modules and study some of their properties. We begin with the definition of ‘cyclic modules’. Throughout this section, we assume that \(R\) is a commutative ring with unity and \(A\) is an \(R\)-algebra. All modules are right \(A\)-modules.
    🔗

    Definition 3.2.1. Cyclic module.

    Let \(A\) be an \(R\)-algebra and \(M\) be a right \(A\)-module. We say that \(M\) is a cyclic module if there exists \(m\in M\) such that \(M=mA\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Lemma 3.2.2.

    Let \(A\) be an \(R\)-algebra and \(M\) be a right \(A\)-module which is cyclic. Then,
    1. \(M\simeq A/\Ann{A}{M}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(N\) is a right ideal of \(A\) then, \(A/N\) is a cyclic right \(A\)-module.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Let \(N\) be a right ideal of \(A\text{.}\) Put \(B=\{a\in A:aN\subseteq N\}\text{.}\) Then, \(B\) is a subalgebra of \(A\) containing \(N\text{.}\) Furthermore, \(\End{A}{A/N}\simeq B/N\) as right \(B\)-modules.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    1. Suppose that \(M=mA\text{.}\) Define a map \(\varphi\colon A\to M\) by \(a\mapsto m\cdot a\text{.}\) As \(M\) is cyclic, the map is surjective and its kernel is precisely \(\Ann{A}{M}\text{.}\) The first isomorphism theorem for modules gives the required isomorphism.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Checking \(A/N\) is a right \(A\)-module is left to the reader. Consider the submodule of \(N\) generated by \(1+N\text{.}\) Given any \(a+N\in A/N\) we have \(a+N=(1+N)\cdot a\text{,}\) and therefore, \(A/N\simeq (1+N)(A/N)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. We first check that \(B\) is a subalgebra of \(A\) containing \(N\text{.}\) For \(b\in B\subseteq A\text{,}\) \(n\in N\text{,}\) and \(r\in R\) we have
      \begin{equation*} (b\cdot r)n =b(n\cdot r), \text{as }n\in N\subseteq A\text{ and using algebra condition }\knowl{./knowl/xref/eqn-algebra.html}{(1.1.1)}. \end{equation*}
      As \(N\) is a right ideal of \(A\) and hence \(n\cdot r\in N\text{.}\) This shows that \(b\cdot r\in B\text{.}\) We leave to the reader to check other conditions for \(B\) to be a subalgebra of \(A\text{.}\)
      🔗
      Note that \(A/N\) is a right \(A\)-module and so is \(\End{A}{A/N}\text{.}\) As \(B\hookrightarrow A\) is a subalgebra, \(\End{A}{A/N}\) is also a right \(B\)-module. We define \(\varphi\colon\End{A}{A/N}\to B/N\) by \(f\mapsto f(1)+N\text{.}\) We check that \(\varphi\) is a \(B\)-module homomorphism. For \(f,g\in\End{A}{A/N}\text{,}\) and \(b,b'\in B\) we have
      \begin{align*} \varphi(f\cdot b+g\cdot b')\amp =(f\cdot b)(1)+(g\cdot b')(1)+N\\ \amp =(f(1)\cdot b)+N+(g(1)\cdot b')+N\\ \amp =\varphi(f)\cdot b+\varphi(g)\cdot b'. \end{align*}
      For \(b+N\in B/N\) consider \(\ell_b\colon A/N\to A/N\) given by \(\ell_b(a+N)=ba+N\text{,}\) i.e., coset representative \(a\) maps to a coset representative \(ba\text{.}\) We check the well-definedness of this map. Suppose that \(a\equiv a'\mod N\text{,}\) say \(a'=a+n\) for some \(n\in N\text{.}\) We obtain the following:
      \begin{align*} \ell_b(a'+N) \amp=\ell_b((a+n)+N) \\ \amp=\left(b(a+n)\right)+N \\ \amp=(ba+bn)+N \\ \amp=ba+N\text{ (by the definition of }B,\, bn\in N) \\ \amp=\ell_b(a+N) \end{align*}
      Suppose now that \(b'=b+n\) for some \(n\in N\text{.}\) We check that \(\ell_b=\ell_{b'}\text{.}\)
      \begin{align*} \ell_{b'}(a+N) \amp=\ell_{b+n}(a+N) \\ \amp=((b+n)a)+N \\ \amp=(ba+na)+N \\ \amp=ba+N\text{ (as }na\in N\text{, since }N\text{ is a right ideal of }A) \\ \amp=\ell_b(a+N) \end{align*}
      Furthermore, the map \(\ell_b\) is \(A\)-linear. Indeed, for \(a,a'\in A\) we have
      \begin{equation*} \ell_b\left((a+N)\cdot a'\right)=\ell_b(aa'+N)=(baa')+N=\ell_b(a+N)\cdot a'. \end{equation*}
      Therefore, we ge the map \(\psi\colon B/N\to\End{A}{A/N}\) given by \(b+N\mapsto\ell_b\text{.}\) One may check that \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are mutually inverse homomorphisms of \(B\)-modules.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.2.3.

    1. Every cyclic group is a cyclic \(\Z\)-module.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Suppose that \(T\colon\R^2\to\R^2\) be an \(\R\)-linear map defined by \(Te_1=e_1\) and \(Te_2=e_1+e_2\text{.}\) We consider \(\R^2\) as a module over \(\R[x]\) via \(T\text{.}\) Note that \(T-\unit\neq 0\) but \((T-\unit)^2=0\text{.}\) In fact \((T-\unit)e_2=e_1\) and hence, \(\{e_2,(T-\unit)e_2\}\) is a basis of \(\R^2\text{.}\) This shows that \(\R^2\) is a cyclic \(\R[x]\)-module generated by \(e_2\text{.}\) By Lemma 3.2.2, we have \(\R^2\simeq \R[x]/(x-1)^2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Let \(A\) be an \(R\)-algebra. Consider \(A\) as a right \(A\)-module over itself. Then, the cyclic submodules of \(A\) are precisely the right principal ideal of \(A\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.2.4. Simple module.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with unity, and \(A\) be an \(R\)-algebra. A right \(A\)-module \(N\) is said to be simple if \(N\) is nonzero and only submodules of \(N\) are \(0\) and \(N\) itself.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.2.5. Minimal right ideal of matrix algebra.

    Suppose that \(D\) is a division algebra over a field \(F\text{,}\) and that \(M_n(D)\) is \(n\times n\) matrix algebra over \(D\text{.}\) Consider the following set.
    \begin{equation*} M_1=\left\{\begin{pmatrix}d_{11}\amp d_{12}\amp\cdots\amp d_{1n}\\0\amp 0\amp\cdots\amp 0\\\vdots\amp\vdots\amp\ddots\amp\vdots\\0\amp 0\amp\cdots\amp 0\end{pmatrix}:d_{1i}\in D\right\} \end{equation*}
    The \(M_1\) is a right ideal of \(M_n(D)\text{.}\) We consider \(M_1\) as a right \(M_n(D)\)-module. We claim that \(M_1\) is a simple right \(M_n(D)\)-module. Suppose that \(0\neq A=(a_{ij})\in M_1\text{,}\) say \(a_{1k}\neq 0\text{.}\) As \(D\) is a division algebra, there exists \(b_k\in D\) such that \(1=b_ka_{1k}=a_{1k}b_k\text{.}\) Assume that \(E_{\ell k}\) to be the matrix with \((\ell,k)\)-th entry \(1\) and all other entries \(0\text{.}\) One check that \(A\cdot E_{k k}b_k=E_{1k}\in AM_n(D)\text{.}\) This shows that for \(d_{11},d_{12},\ldots,d_{1n}\in D\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^n E_{1k}d_{1k}=\begin{pmatrix}d_{11}\amp d_{12}\amp\cdots\amp d_{1n}\\0\amp 0\amp\cdots\amp 0\\\vdots\amp\vdots\amp\ddots\amp\vdots\\0\amp 0\amp\cdots\amp 0\end{pmatrix}\in AM_n(D). \end{equation*}
    Therefore, \(AM_n(D)=M_1\text{,}\) i.e., \(M_1\) is a simple right \(M_n(D)\)-module.
    🔗
    The above arguments show that \(M\) is a minimal right ideal of \(M_n(D)\text{,}\) and that for any \(0\neq A\in M_1\) we have \(M_n(D)\)-module isomorphism \(AM_n(D)\simeq M_1\text{.}\) Furthermore, if we denote by \(M_i\) as the set of matrices in \(M_n(D)\) whose only nonzero row can be the \(i\)-th row, then each \(M_i\) is a minimal right ideal of \(M_n(D)\text{.}\) Note that \(M_n(D)=\bigoplus_{i=1}^n M_i\text{.}\) One may also check that \(M_i\simeq M_j\) for every \(i,j\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 3.2.6.

    Let \(M\) be a nonzero right \(A\)-module. Following conditions are equivalent.
    1. The module \(M\) is simple;
      🔗
      🔗
    2. For any nonzero \(m\in M\text{,}\) \(M\simeq mA\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The module \(M\simeq A_A/\mathfrak{m}\) for some maximal right ideal \(\mathfrak{m}\) of \(A\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Assume that \(M\) is simple and that \(m\in M\) is nonzero. Thus, the submodule generated by \(m\text{,}\) \(mA\) is a nonzero submodule of \(M\text{.}\) Thus, \(M=mA\text{.}\) Conversely, if \(M=mA\) for any nonzero element \(m\in M\) then, for any nonzero submodule \(N\leq M\) and \(0\neq n\in N\text{,}\) we have \(M=nA\leq N\leq M\text{,}\) i.e., \(N=M\text{,}\) i.e., \(M\) is simple.
    🔗
    We fix a nonzero element \(m\in M\) and define a map \(f\colon A\to mA\) by \(a\mapsto m\cdot a\text{.}\) This is a surjective \(A\)-module homomorphism. The kernel of \(f\) is
    \begin{equation*} \ker f=\{a\in A:m\cdot a=0\}=\Ann{A}{m}. \end{equation*}
    By Lemma 3.1.4, \(\ker f\) is a right ideal of \(A\text{.}\) By the isomorphism theorem, \(A_A/\ker f\simeq mA\text{.}\) By the Correspondence theorem, submodules of \(mA\) corresponds to right ideals of \(A\) containing \(\ker f\text{.}\) Thus, \(M\) is simple if and only if there are no proper right ideals of \(A\) containing \(\ker f\text{.}\) This proves the equivalence of (1) and (3).
    🔗
    🔗
    Using the above Proposition 3.2.6 we obtain the following important lemma. Its proof is left to the reader.
    🔗

    Lemma 3.2.7. Schur’s lemma.

    Let \(M\) and \(N\) be right \(A\)-modules, and \(f\colon M\to N\) be a nonzero \(A\)-module homomorphism.
    1. If \(M\) is simple then, \(f\) is injective.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(N\) is simple then, \(f\) is surjective.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 3.2.8.

    If \(M\) and \(N\) are simple \(A\)-modules then either \(M\simeq N\) or \(\Hom{A}{M}{N}=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.2.9. Semisimple module.

    A right \(A\)-module \(M\) is said to be semisimple if \(M\) is a direct sum of simple \(A\)-modules.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.2.10. Matrix algebra is semisimple.

    We have shown in Example 3.2.5 that \(M_n(D)\) is a semisimple \(M_n(D)\)-module.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.2.11. Indecomposable module.

    A right \(A\)-module \(M\) is said to be indecomposable if \(M\neq 0\) and that if \(M=P\oplus Q\) for some submodules \(P,Q\) then, either \(P=0\) or \(Q=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.2.12. Indecomposable module need not be simple.

    Every simple \(A\)-module is indecomposable. However, the converse need not be true, as shown below.
    🔗
    Consider \(\Z/4\Z\) as a \(\Z\)-module. As the subgroup generated by \(2\) is a proper nontrivial submodule of \(M\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\Z/4\Z\) is not simple. Recall that there are, up to isomorphism, only two groups of order \(4\text{,}\) viz., \(\Z/4\Z\) and \(\Z/2\Z\times\Z/2\Z\text{.}\) This shows that \(\Z/4\Z\) is indecomposable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 3.2.13.

    Let \(N\) be a semisimple right \(A\)-module. The following conditions are equivalent.
    1. The module \(N\) is simple;
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\End{A}{N}\) is division algebra;
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The module \(N\) is indecomposable.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(N\) is simple. By Lemma 3.2.7, every nonzero \(A\)-module endomorphism is invertible, i.e., \(\End{A}{N}\) is division.
    🔗
    Assume that \(\End{A}{N}\) is division algebra, and that \(N+P\oplus Q\) for some \(A\)-submodules \(P,Q\) of \(N\text{.}\) The propjection \(\pi\colon N\to P\) (resp., \unit_N-\pi\colon N\to Q) is an \(A\)-module homomorphism.
