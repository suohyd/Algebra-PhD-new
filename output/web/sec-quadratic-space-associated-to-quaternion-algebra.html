    Section 2.4 Quadratic space associated to quaternion algebra

    Throughout this section we assume that \(F\) is a field with \({\rm ch}(F)\neq 2\text{.}\) The set of nonzero elements of \(F\) is denoted by \(\dot{F}\text{.}\)

    Definition 2.4.1. Symmetric bilinear form associated to quaternion algebra.

    Let \(Q=(a,b)_F\) be a quaternion algebra over \(F\text{.}\) The following map is a symmetric bilinear form associated to \(Q\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} b_Q\colon Q\times Q\to F\quad\text{given by}\quad (x,y)\mapsto\tfrac{1}{2}\left(x\gamma(y)+y\gamma(x)\right)=\tfrac{1}{2}\Trd{Q}\left(x\gamma(y)\right) \end{equation*}

    We keep notations of DefinitionÂ 2.4.1 above. The quadratic form \(q\) associated to \(b_Q\) is given by \(q(x)=b_Q(x,x)\) for \(x\in Q\text{.}\)

    Lemma 2.4.6.

    Keep notations of DefinitionÂ 2.4.1. The matrix associated to \(b_Q\) is a diagonal matrix \({\rm diag}(1,-a,-b,ab)\text{,}\) and \(b_Q\) is regular.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    An easy calculations show that \(\{1,i,j,k\}\) is an orthogonal basis with respect to \(b_Q\text{.}\) Hence, the matrix associated to \(b_Q\) is a diagonal matrix. More precisely, we get the following.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix}b_Q(1,1)\amp b_Q(1,i)\amp b_Q(1,j)\amp b_Q(1,k)\\b_Q(i,1)\amp b_Q(i,i)\amp b_Q(i,j)\amp b_Q(i,k)\\b_Q(j,1)\amp b_Q(j,i)\amp b_Q(j,j)\amp b_Q(j,k)\\b_Q(k,1)\amp b_Q(k,i)\amp b_Q(k,j)\amp b_Q(k,k)\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1\amp 0\amp 0\amp 0\\0\amp -a\amp 0\amp 0\\0\amp 0\amp -b\amp 0\\0\amp 0\amp 0\amp ab\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
    As \(a,b\in\dot{F}\text{,}\) we get that \(b_Q\) is regular.

    Theorem 2.4.7.

    Let \(F\) be a field of characteristic different from \(2\text{.}\) Suppose that \(Q=(a,b)_F\) and \(Q'=(a',b')_F\) are quaternion algebras over \(F\text{.}\) Then, \(Q\simeq Q'\) as an \(F\)-algebras if and only if there exists an \(F\)-linear isomorphism \(\ell\colon Q_+\to Q'_+\) such that \(\Nrd{Q'}(\ell(x))=\Nrd{Q}(x)\) for all \(x\in Q_+\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    We use LemmaÂ 2.1.11 without explicit mention. We also denote the canonical involution on both \(Q\) and \(Q'\) by \(\gamma\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose that \(\phi\colon Q\to Q'\) is an \(F\)-algebra isomophism. For \(x\in Q_+\text{,}\) \(\phi(x)\not\in F\) as \(x\not\in F\text{.}\) Furthermore, \(x^2\in F\) implies that \(\left(\phi(x)\right)^2=\phi(x^2)\in F\text{.}\) We thus get that \(\phi(x)\in Q_+'\) for every \(x\in Q_+\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\ell=\phi|_{Q_+}\) is an \(F\)-linear map from \(Q_+\) to \(Q_+'\text{.}\) As \(\phi\) is injective, the map \(\ell\) is injective as well. Now assume that \(y\in Q_+'\text{.}\) Then there exists some \(x\in Q\) such that \(\phi(x)=y\text{.}\) As \(y\not\in F\) and \(y^2\in F\) we get that \(x\not\in F\) and that \(\phi(x^2)\in F\text{,}\) i.e., \(\phi(x^2)=\alpha\in F\) for some \(\alpha\text{.}\) Thus, \(\phi\left(x^2-\alpha\cdot 1\right)=0\text{,}\) i.e., \(x^2\in F\text{.}\) Hence, \(x\in Q_+\) and thus, \(\ell\) is surjective as well. It follows that \(\ell\colon Q_+\to Q_+'\) is an \(F\)-isomorphism. Finally for \(x\in Q_+\text{,}\) we have \(\Nrd{Q}(x)=-x^2\in F\) and thus,
    \begin{equation*} \Nrd{Q_+'}(\ell(x))=-\ell(x)^2=-\ell\left(x^2\right)=-x^2=\Nrd{Q}(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Now assume there is an \(F\)-linear isomorphism \(\ell\colon Q_+\to Q_+'\) such that \(\Nrd{Q'}(\ell(x))=\Nrd{Q}(x)\) for all \(x\in Q_+\text{.}\) Define \(\phi\colon Q\to Q'\) by \(1\mapsto 1\text{,}\) \(i\mapsto\ell(i)\text{,}\) \(j\mapsto\ell(j)\text{,}\) and \(k\mapsto\ell(k)\text{.}\) Extend \(\phi\) linearly to all \(Q\text{.}\) The reader may check that \(\phi\) is an \(F\)-algebra isomorphism. Here we only check that \(\phi(i)\phi(j)=-\phi(j)\phi(i)\text{.}\) First observe the following.
    \begin{align*} b_{Q'}\left(\ell(i-j),\ell(i-j)\right) \amp= b_{Q'}\left(\ell(i),\ell(i)\right)-2b_{Q'}\left(\ell(i),\ell(j)\right)+b_{Q'}\left(\ell(j),\ell(j)\right)\\ \amp=b_{Q}\left(i,i\right)-2b_{Q'}\left(\ell(i),\ell(j)\right)+b_{Q}\left(j,j\right) \end{align*}
    On the other hand,
    \begin{align*} b_{Q'}\left(\ell(i-j),\ell(i-j)\right)\amp=b_{Q}\left(i-j,i-j\right) \\ \amp=b_{Q}\left(i,i\right)-2b_{Q}\left(i,j\right)+b_{Q}\left(j,j\right). \end{align*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation} b_{Q'}\left(\ell(i),\ell(j)\right)=b_{Q}\left(i,j\right).\tag{2.4.1} \end{equation}
    Using \(\phi(i),\phi(j)\in Q_+'\) and the associated symmetric bilinear form \(b_{Q'}\) we get the following.
    \begin{align*} b_{Q'}(\phi(i),\phi(j)) \amp= \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\phi(i)\gamma(\phi(j))+\phi(j)\gamma(\phi(i))\right)\amp \\ \amp=-\tfrac{1}{2}\left(\phi(i)\phi(j)+\phi(j)\phi(i)\right)\amp\text{ as }\phi(i),\phi(j)\in Q_+' \\ \amp=b_Q(i,j) \amp \text{ by }\knowl{./knowl/xref/eq-bilinear-forms-equal-on-i-j.html}{\text{(2.4.1)}}\\ \amp=0 \amp \end{align*}
    This shows that \(\phi(i)\phi(j)=-\phi(j)\phi(i)\text{.}\)
