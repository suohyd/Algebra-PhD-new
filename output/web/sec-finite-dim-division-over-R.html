    Section 2.3 Finite-dimensional division algebras over real and complex numbers

    We classify finite-dimensional division algebras over \(\R\text{.}\) We begin with the following definition.

    Definition 2.3.1. Finite-dimension division algebra.

    Let \(F\) be a field. An \(F\)-algebra \(D\) is said to be finite-dimensional division algebra if \(D\) is finite dimensional as an \(F\)-vector space, and every nonzero element of \(D\) is invertible.

    Lemma 2.3.2.

    Let \(D\) be a finite-dimensional division algebra over \(\R\text{.}\) Then, for every element \(x\in D\) there exists \(a\in\R\) such that \(x^2+ax\in\R\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Let \(D\) be an \(n\)-dimensional division algebra over \(\R\text{.}\) Then, the set \(\{1,x,x^2,\ldots,x^n\}\) is linearly dependent over \(\R\text{.}\) Thus, there exists \(a_0,a_1,\ldots,a_n\in\R\text{,}\) not all zero, such that \(f(x)=a_0+a_1x+\cdots+a_nx^n=0\text{.}\) Since \(D\) is a division algebra, we must have \(a_n\neq 0\text{,}\) and hence by dividing by \(a_n^{-1}\text{,}\) we may assume that the polynomial is monic. Over \(\R\text{,}\) the polynomial \(f(t)\) will splits into linear and quadratic factors. Say
    \begin{equation*} f(t)=(t-\alpha_1)\cdots(t-\alpha_r)(t^2+b_1t+c_1)\cdots(t^2+b_st+c_s). \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    \begin{equation*} 0=f(x)=(x-\alpha_1)\cdots(x-\alpha_r)(x^2+b_1x+c_1)\cdots(x^2+b_sx+c_s). \end{equation*}
    Since \(D\) is a division algebra, one of the factors is zero. This proves the lemma.

    Lemma 2.3.3.

    Let \(D\) be a finite-dimensional division algebra over \(\R\text{.}\) Let \(V=\{x\in D\mid x^2\in\R,\,x^2\leq 0\}\text{.}\) Then, \(V\) is an \(\R\)-vector subspace of \(D\text{,}\) and \(D=\R\oplus V\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Note that if \(d\in D\setminus V\) and \(d^2\in\R\) then, \(d^2>0\) and hence there exists \(r\in\R\) such that \(d^2=r^2\text{.}\) Thus, \(0=(d-r)(d+r)\in D\text{,}\) i.e., \(d=\pm r\in\R\text{.}\) Now assume that \(u,v\in V\text{.}\) We show that \(u+v\in V\text{.}\) We further assume that \(u,v\) are \(\R\)-linearly independent. We claim that \(\{1,u,v\}\) are \(\R\)-linearly independent. If there are \(r,s,t\in\R\) with \(ur+vs+t=0\) then, \(0=(ur+vs+t)^2\text{.}\) This gives \(stv\in\R\text{.}\) This implies that either \(s=0\) or \(t=0\text{.}\) One may check that this gives \(r=s=t=0\text{.}\) We have thus shown that \(\{1,u,v\}\) are linearly independent. By LemmaÂ 2.3.2, there exists \(a,b\in\R\) such that
    \begin{equation*} (u+v)^2 + a(u+v)\in\R\quad\text{and}\quad (u-v)^2 + b(u-v)\in\R. \end{equation*}
    While \((u+v)^2+(u-v)^2=2u^2+2v^2\in\R\text{.}\) This gives \(a(u+v)+b(u-v)\in\R\text{.}\) Since \(\{1,u,v\}\) are \(\R\)-linearly independent, we must have \(a=b=0\text{.}\) Thus, \((u+v)\in V\text{.}\)

    We leave it to the reader to check that \(V\) is closed under scalar multiplication. This shows that \(V\) is an \(\R\)-vector subspace of \(D\text{.}\) Further, check that \(\R\cap V=0\text{.}\)

    For \(d\in D\setminus\R\) there exists \(a\in\R\) such that \(d^2+ad\in\R\text{.}\) Note that \(d+a/2\in V\) and hence, \(d=-a/2+(d+a/2)\in\R+V\text{.}\) This shows that \(D=\R\oplus V\text{.}\)

    Lemma 2.3.4.

    Let \(D\) be a finite-dimensional division algebra over \(\R\text{.}\) If the dimension of \(D\) is \(\geq 3\text{,}\) then there exist \(i,j,k\in V\) such that \(i^2=j^2=k^2=-1\text{,}\) \(ij=k=-ji\text{,}\) and \(\{1,i,j,k\}\) are linearly independent over \(\R\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    By LemmaÂ 2.3.3, \(\dim_\R V\geq 2\text{.}\) Suppose that \(u,v\in V\) are linearly independent over \(\R\text{.}\) Consider the element \(w=v-\tfrac{vu+uv}{2u^2}u\text{.}\) Set
    \begin{equation*} i=\frac{1}{\sqrt{-w^2}}w,\quad j=\frac{1}{\sqrt{-u^2}}u,\quad\text{and}\quad k=ij. \end{equation*}
    It is left to the reader to check that \(i,j,k\) defined above satisfies (2.1.1). To check the linear independence of \(\{1,i,j,k\}\) we consider \(x_i\in\R\) such that \(x_0+x_1i+x_2j+x_3k=0\text{.}\) However,
    \begin{equation*} \left(x_0+x_1i+x_2j+x_3k\right)\left(x_0-x_1i-x_2j-x_3k\right)=x_0^2+x_1^2+x_2^2+x_3^2\in\R, \end{equation*}
    implies that \(x_i=0\) for all \(i\text{.}\)

    Lemma 2.3.6.

    There are no proper nontrivial finite-dimentional division algebras over \(\C\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Suppose that \(D\) is a finite-dimensional division algebra over \(\C\text{.}\) Let \(d\in D\text{.}\) As \(D\) is finite-dimensional, \(d\) is an algebraic element over \(\C\text{.}\) Thus, \(d\in\C\) because \(\C\) is an algebraically closed field. This shows that \(D=\C\text{.}\)
