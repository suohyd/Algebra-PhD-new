    Section 1.6 Quaternion algebra

    Throughout this section we assume that \(F\) is a field with \({\rm ch}(F)\neq 2\text{.}\) The set of nonzero elements of \(F\) is denoted by \(\dot{F}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 1.6.1.

    Let \(a,b\in\dot{F}\text{.}\) The quaternion algebra \((a,b)_F\) or \(\left(\tfrac{a,b}{F}\right)\) is the four-dimensional \(F\)-vector space with basis \(\{1,i,j,k\}\) and multiplication defined by the following relations and with \(1\) as unity of \((a,b)_F\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation} i^2 = a\cdot 1, \quad j^2 = b\cdot 1, \quad ij = k = -ji.\tag{1.6.1} \end{equation}
    We assume the associativity of multiplication and extend the multiplication to all elements of \((a,b)_F\) by distributivity and linearity.
    ðŸ”—
    We get the following relations.
    \begin{equation} k^2 = -ab\cdot 1, \quad jk = -kj = -bi, \quad ki = -ik = -aj.\tag{1.6.2} \end{equation}

    The algebra \((-1,-1)_\R\) is Hamiltonion quaternion algebra over the field of real numbers \(\R\text{.}\) It is usually denoted by \(\mathbb{H}\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.6.4.

    Let \(A\) be an \(F\)-algebra of finite dimension as an \(F\)-vector space. We say that \(A\) is a simple algebra if the only two-sided ideals of \(A\) are \(0\) and \(A\text{.}\)

    Proposition 1.6.5.

    Let \(A=(a,b)_F\) be a quaternion algebra over \(F\text{.}\) Then, \(A\) is a simple algebra with center \(F\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Let \(I\) be a nonzero two-sided ideal of \(A\text{.}\) We show that \(I=A\text{.}\) Let \(x\in I\) be a nonzero element. Then, we can write \(x=x_01+x_1i+x_2j+x_3k\) for some \(x_0,x_1,x_2,x_3\in F\text{.}\) For \(x,y\in A\) we write \([x,y]=xy-yx\text{.}\) Then, we have the following relations.
    \begin{align*} [i,x] \amp=2ax_3j+2x_2k\in I \\ [j,x] \amp=-2bx_3i-2x_1k\in I\\ [k,x] \amp=2bx_2i-2ax_1j\in I \end{align*}
    Now \([j,[i,x]]=-4bx_2i\text{,}\) \([k,[j,x]]=4abx_3j\text{,}\) and \([i,[k,x]]=-4ax_1k\text{.}\) So, if one of \(x_1,x_2,x_3\) is nonzero then, \(I\) will contain a unit, and thus, \(I=A\text{.}\) If \(x_1=x_2=x_3=0\text{,}\) then \(x=x_01\in I\) for some nonzero \(x_0\in F\text{.}\) Thus, \(1=x_0^{-1}x\in I\text{,}\) and hence, \(I=A\text{.}\)

    If \(x\in Z(A)\) then we must have \([i,x]=[j,x]=[k,x]=0\text{.}\) This implies that \(x=x_0\cdot 1\text{.}\) Thus, \(Z(A)=F\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.6.6. Finite-dimension division algebra.

    An \(F\)-algebra \(D\) is said to be finite-dimensional division algebra if \(D\) is finite dimensional as an \(F\)-vector space, and every nonzero element of \(D\) is invertible.

    ðŸ”—
    Now we classify finite-dimensional division algebras over \(\R\text{.}\) We begin with the following lemma.
    ðŸ”—

    Lemma 1.6.7.

    Let \(D\) be a finite-dimensional division algebra over \(\R\text{.}\) Then, for every element \(x\in D\) there exists \(a\in\R\) such that \(x^2+ax\in\R\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Let \(D\) be an \(n\)-dimensional division algebra over \(\R\text{.}\) Then, the set \(\{1,x,x^2,\ldots,x^n\}\) is linearly dependent over \(\R\text{.}\) Thus, there exists \(a_0,a_1,\ldots,a_n\in\R\text{,}\) not all zero, such that \(f(x)=a_0+a_1x+\cdots+a_nx^n=0\text{.}\) Since \(D\) is a division algebra, we must have \(a_n\neq 0\text{,}\) and hence by dividing by \(a_n^{-1}\text{,}\) we may assume that the polynomial is monic. Over \(\R\text{,}\) the polynomial \(f(t)\) will splits into linear and quadratic factors. Say
    \begin{equation*} f(t)=(t-\alpha_1)\cdots(t-\alpha_r)(t^2+b_1t+c_1)\cdots(t^2+b_st+c_s). \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    \begin{equation*} 0=f(x)=(x-\alpha_1)\cdots(x-\alpha_r)(x^2+b_1x+c_1)\cdots(x^2+b_sx+c_s). \end{equation*}
    Since \(D\) is a division algebra, one of the factors is zero. This proves the lemma.

    ðŸ”—

    Lemma 1.6.8.

    Let \(D\) be a finite-dimensional division algebra over \(\R\text{.}\) Let \(V=\{x\in D\mid x^2\in\R,\,x^2\leq 0\}\text{.}\) Then, \(V\) is an \(\R\)-vector subspace of \(D\text{,}\) and \(D=\R\oplus V\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Note that if \(d\in D\setminus V\) and \(d^2\in\R\) then, \(d^2>0\) and hence there exists \(r\in\R\) such that \(d^2=r^2\text{.}\) Thus, \(0=(d-r)(d+r)\in D\text{,}\) i.e., \(d=\pm r\in\R\text{.}\) Now assume that \(u,v\in V\text{.}\) We show that \(u+v\in V\text{.}\) We further assume that \(u,v\) are \(\R\)-linearly independent. We claim that \(\{1,u,v\}\) are \(\R\)-linearly independent. If there are \(r,s,t\in\R\) with \(ur+vs+t=0\) then, \(0=(ur+vs+t)^2\text{.}\) This gives \(stv\in\R\text{.}\) This implies that either \(s=0\) or \(t=0\text{.}\) One may check that this gives \(r=s=t=0\text{.}\) We have thus shown that \(\{1,u,v\}\) are linearly independent. By LemmaÂ 1.6.7, there exists \(a,b\in\R\) such that
    \begin{equation*} (u+v)^2 + a(u+v)\in\R\quad\text{and}\quad (u-v)^2 + b(u-v)\in\R. \end{equation*}
    While \((u+v)^2+(u-v)^2=2u^2+2v^2\in\R\text{.}\) This gives \(a(u+v)+b(u-v)\in\R\text{.}\) Since \(\{1,u,v\}\) are \(\R\)-linearly independent, we must have \(a=b=0\text{.}\) Thus, \((u+v)\in V\text{.}\)

    We leave it to the reader to check that \(V\) is closed under scalar multiplication. This shows that \(V\) is an \(\R\)-vector subspace of \(D\text{.}\) Further, check that \(\R\cap V=0\text{.}\)

    For \(d\in D\setminus\R\) there exists \(a\in\R\) such that \(d^2+ad\in\R\text{.}\) Note that \(d+a/2\in V\) and hence, \(d=-a/2+(d+a/2)\in\R+V\text{.}\) This shows that \(D=\R\oplus V\text{.}\)
